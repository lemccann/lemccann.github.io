Most of the books I enjoy reading for fun are “heavy” reads. Unsurprisingly, this preference has become unwieldy for someone whose job is focused on development, economics, and policy. At some point I realized I needed to treat my reading like I do my running. I incorporate “rest and recovery” books after I read something heavy. These are almost exclusively rom coms, because I like the predictability and I need something that isn't going to break me further. I can generally tell within 20 pages how many rom coms I will need to consume after reading something heavy. My current read, <em>Blood Meridian</em> by Cormac McCarthy, will require at least three.

    <div class="books-title">East of Eden</div>

    <p class="books-meta"><strong>John Steinbeck (1952)</strong></p>

    <p class="books-blurb">
This is my all-time favorite book. <em>The Grapes of Wrath</em> obviously appeals to my policy interests topically (and who doesn't love the jalopy vignette in chapter 7?), but <em>East of Eden</em> is imprinted in my mind. I am also a sucker for biblical allusion. 
It is fitting that I read this a couple of months before starting graduate school. Much like rational choice theory in any policy process class, I think of this book as a prompt. Steinbeck poses a clean, simple theoretical model: humans will always face decisions between good and evil. Obviously, few outcomes are purely “good” or “evil,” but as boundedly rational humans, we tend to dichotomize them, often to our own detriment. Lee’s (perfect) monologue explaining the Hebrew translation of  תִּמְשָׁל “timshel” highlights the key assumption of this model, which is the gift of choice. </p>  

<blockquote> “Don’t you see? . . . The American Standard translation orders men to triumph over sin, and you call sin ignorance. The King James translation makes a promise in ‘Thou shalt,’ meaning that men will surely triumph over sin. But the Hebrew word, the word timshel—‘Thou mayest’—that gives a choice. It might be the most important word in the world. That says the way is open.”</blockquote>




<p>Everyone (read: Reddit) says “If you like Steinbeck, you’ll like Cormac McCarthy.” I see why people say this, but Steinbeck’s central thesis of choice is exactly what I find lacking in McCarthy’s characters. There is no choice, and therefore no hope. McCarthy does not love his characters. He does not want better for them. It is exhausting to live in McCarthy's world.
</p>
